I've often wondered how the Fighting Illini’s historical success actually stacks up
against the "Blue Bloods." We think of ourselves as a premier basketball
school, but do the numbers back that up? We might not make the Final Four
every season, but the expectation is always there.

To separate perception from reality, I dove into the hard data. The result
is a comprehensive map of the modern college basketball landscape, divided into
five distinct tiers of performance. While the full technical breakdown is
available in the [methodology below](#methodology), let’s start at the top and
see who actually rules the court.

## Tiers

{: #tiers}

We've clustered teams into 5 tiers based on their historical NCAA tournament
performance. For this discussion, we're considering tournament data since
the 1984-1985 season. You can read more in the [methodology](#methodology)
section about why that date range was chosen.

### Tier 1

The absolute elite. Programs in this tier haven’t just won—they’ve
dominated, averaging **4.2 NCAA titles** and **10.4 Final Four appearances**
per school. To even earn a seat at this table, a program must have at
least **three titles** and **eight Final Four trips** on their modern resume.

These schools aren't just "Blue Bloods," they are the definitive measures of
greatness in the 64-team era.

Duke sits at the absolute pinnacle. They aren’t just Rank 1; they
are the standard. The mathematical gap between Duke and North Carolina
(Rank 2) is so vast that it rivals the entire distance between lower
tiers.

Notably, UCLA—often cited as the ultimate "Blue Blood"—finds itself in Tier 2.
This is a direct consequence of our 1985 cutoff. While the Bruins own the
20th century, their post-1985 resume (one title in 1995) simply doesn't
pack the same historical weight in the modern era as the five names above.

### Tier 2

But if Tier 1 is the exclusive penthouse of college basketball, Tier 2 is the
spacious upper floor. These programs are perennial threats, winning
**nearly two titles** on average and reaching the Final Four roughly
**every seven years**. Every team in this group has secured at least
**one championship** and made at least **four total Final Four appearances**.

Florida occupies the #6 spot, making them effectively the "Best
of the Rest." However, there is a significant mathematical gulf between them
and Kentucky (Rank 5). Florida's historical score represents a
nearly 30% drop-off from the Tier 1 floor—a sobering reminder of just how
difficult it is to break into the true elite.

### Tier 3

Moving deeper into the rankings, we find the consistent staples of the tournament:
Tier 3. While championships are rarer here—only **five teams** in this
group have hoisted a trophy since 1985—every single program has proven they
can navigate the gauntlet to reach the **Final Four**.

These programs reach the tournament with nearly the same frequency as Tier 2
(**25.3** appearances vs **29.6**) and often carry similar expectations
into March (**5.4** average seed vs **4.9**). The divergence only happens
at the highest level—where the ability to close out semi-finals and national
championships separates the "Contenders" from the "Elites."

### Tier 4

The transition to Tier 4 marks a shift from consistent contenders to
those fighting for a breakthrough. Only **one team** in this group has won a
title in the modern era, and just over **half** have ever reached a
Final Four.

This category represents a massive logjam of programs waiting for their turn
in the sun. Notable examples include Baylor (Rank 28) and
Houston (Rank 39). Both have had incredible recent runs, but they
illustrate the **"Glass Ceiling"** of historical analysis: despite their
recent dominance, they still trail Tier 3 mainstays like Memphis in
total historical weight. For these programs, the goal isn't just one title—it's
decades of sustained success to bridge the gap.

## The Illini Corner

{: #the-illini-corner}

As an Illinois fan, it's natural to wonder why the Fighting Illini sit at
**Rank 19**, in Tier 3. The Illini have been a model of consistency, making the
tournament **28 times** since 1985 and reaching the Final Four **three times**—including
the legendary 1989 "Flyin' Illini," the 2005 Deron Williams and Dee Brown-led
squad, and this year’s "Balkan 5."

In the expanded Big 10, Illinois currently ranks **6th** in historical
tournament performance, trailing only Michigan, Michigan State,
UCLA, Indiana, and Purdue.

**What if the Illini had won in 2005?**

It’s the question that keeps Champaign awake at night. If that 2005
runner-up finish had been a Championship, Illinois would have vaulted to
**Rank 15**, leapfrogging both Purdue and Indiana. Yet, even with
that extra hardware, they would remain firmly in Tier 3—a testament to the
massive amount of "historical gravity" required to break into the top
two tiers. To even graze the bottom of Tier 2, the Illini would have
needed championships in 1989, 2005, and 2026 (which would
have landed them at **Rank 12**).

## Methodology

{: #methodology}

Of course, these tiers didn’t just appear out of thin air. Behind the
rankings is a rigorous process designed to isolate performance in the most
competitive environment in sports. We focused exclusively on NCAA
tournament results from the **1984-1985 season** onward—the dawn of the
modern 64-team era.[^8] While history stretches back further, the field
expansion created a fundamental shift in the difficulty and structure
of the path to a title.

Other notable cutoffs we could have chosen include:

- 1970-1971 season: The NCAA decreed that teams must accept invites to the NCAA
  tournament, making it the definitive national championship.[^8]
- 1985-1986 season: The NCAA adopted the shot clock.[^9]
- 1986-1987 season: The 3-pt line was introduced nationally.[^7]

To ensure a balanced perspective, we’ve applied two distinct scoring systems to
evaluate these performances, utilizing the average of both to determine our
final tiers. Natural groupings were then identified using the Fisher-Jenks
algorithm,[^2]{{page.fn-sep}}[^3]{{page.fn-sep}}[^5] with the optimal number
of clusters determined through Kneedle point detection.[^4]{{page.fn-sep}}[^6]

### Double Scoring System

The first method is the Double Scoring System, which emphasizes elite finishes
by doubling the point value with every successive round:

- Appearance with no wins: 1
- Round of 32 finish: 2
- Sweet 16 finish: 4
- Elite 8 finish: 8
- Final Four finish: 16
- Runner up finish: 32
- Championship finish: 64

### Mike's Scoring System

While Double Scoring rewards the eventual winner, Mike's Scoring System, developed
by Mike Lefever, provides a more granular view of the earlier rounds. This system
factors in tournament seeds for teams that don't secure a win, acknowledging the
difficulty of the road even when it ends early:

- Appearance with no wins:
  - 16-seed: 1
  - 15-seed: 2
  - ...
  - 1-seed: 16
- Round of 32 finish: 20
- Sweet 16 finish: 25
- Elite 8 finish: 50
- Final Four finish: 100
- Runner up finish: 125
- Championship finish: 200
